# Comparative Cases: What Worked Elsewhere to Defeat Gangs

**Last Updated:** 2026-02-14 19:15 EST  
**Status:** Active Research - Evidence-Based Analysis

## Overview
This document analyzes gang/armed group dismantling efforts in comparable contexts to Haiti. Focus on **what specifically worked, what failed, costs, timelines, and prerequisites**. No ideological solutions without evidence.

---

## Case 1: El Salvador - Mass Incarceration Approach (2022-Present)

### Context
- Similar gang structure to Haiti: MS-13 and Barrio 18 dominated country
- Population: 6.3M (similar size to Haiti's 11M)
- President Nayib Bukele declared "state of exception" March 2022

### What Happened (Facts Only):
**Results:**
- **Homicide reduction: >80%** (from ~50/100k to single digits)
- **2024 total homicides: 114** (record low in recent history)
- **Arrests: 84,000+ people detained** (March 2022 - Jan 2025)
- **Incarceration rate: Highest in the world** 
- **Innocent people detained: Est. 8,000 released** (Bukele's own admission, Nov 2024)
- **Wrongful detention estimate: ~50% of detainees** may have no gang connections (human rights lawyers)
- **Prison deaths: 261 reported deaths** in 2024 (torture, denial of medical care, abuse - Cristosal report)

**Methods:**
- Suspended constitutional rights (habeas corpus, right to lawyer, due process)
- Mass arrests without warrants based on appearance/tattoos/neighborhood
- Built mega-prison (CECOT) to hold tens of thousands
- Life sentences without parole for gang membership
- Military deployment to support police

**Costs:**
- Financial: Not publicly disclosed (prison construction, expanded security forces)
- Human rights: Severe - arbitrary detention, torture allegations, family separations
- Democratic institutions: Rule of law ranking fell to 111th of 142 countries (World Justice Project 2024)
- International legitimacy: Condemned by UN, Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International

### Timeline:
- **March 2022:** State of exception declared after 87 people killed in one weekend
- **2022-2023:** Peak arrest period, violence drops dramatically
- **2024:** Bukele wins re-election (despite constitutional prohibition) on security success
- **2025-present:** State of exception still in effect (3+ years)

### What Worked:
1. **Overwhelming force** - Arrested ~1.3% of entire population
2. **Visible security presence** - Military in streets
3. **Disrupted gang command structure** - Leaders isolated in prison
4. **Public support** - Overwhelming approval despite human rights concerns (70-80% approval rating)

### What Failed/Costs:
1. **Due process completely abandoned** - Half of detainees likely innocent
2. **No long-term plan** - What happens when prisons fill up? When state of exception ends?
3. **Prison conditions inhumane** - Death toll rising
4. **Families destroyed** - Innocent people imprisoned for years without trial
5. **Democratic backsliding** - Constitutional violations, authoritarian concentration of power
6. **No root cause address** - Poverty, lack of opportunity unchanged

### Prerequisites for Replication:
- Strong executive willing to ignore constitution/international law
- Sufficient prison capacity (or willingness to build massive new prisons)
- Military/police loyal to executive (no coups, no defections)
- Public willing to trade rights for security
- Ability to withstand international criticism

### Applicability to Haiti:
**Potential:**
- Could dramatically reduce violence in short term
- Haiti has similar gang structure (territorial control, hierarchical)

**Challenges:**
- Haiti has ZERO prison capacity (gangs already control existing prisons)
- Haiti police force is 1/10th the size needed
- No functional judiciary to process even show trials
- International peacekeepers unlikely to participate in mass human rights violations
- Haitian government too weak/fragmented to execute this model
- Would require authoritarian strongman (Haiti has transitional council)

**Verdict:** **High short-term effectiveness, catastrophic human rights costs, requires prerequisites Haiti lacks**

## Case 2: Colombia (Medellín) - Social Urbanism + Security (1990s-2010s)

### Context
- Medellín was "most dangerous city in the world" in early 1990s (homicide rate: 381/100k in 1991)
- Pablo Escobar's Medellín Cartel + paramilitary groups + urban militias controlled slums
- Population: 2M+ city, poor neighborhoods (comunas) isolated on hillsides

### What Happened (Facts Only):
**Results:**
- **Homicide reduction: >90% over 25 years** (381/100k in 1991 → ~20/100k by 2016)
- **"Medellín Miracle"** - transformed from murder capital to model city
- **Tenfold reduction in violence** while improving public services

**Methods (Multi-Pronged):**
1. **Social Urbanism (Integrated Urban Projects - PUI):**
   - Built cable cars (Metrocable) connecting poor hillside neighborhoods to city center
   - Constructed libraries, schools, parks in most violent areas
   - Upgraded public spaces, improved mobility infrastructure
   - Created jobs through construction projects

2. **Security Operations:**
   - Military pacification operations against cartels/paramilitaries
   - Targeted killing of Pablo Escobar (1993)
   - Negotiated demobilization of some armed groups

3. **Political Consistency:**
   - **Sustained 15+ year political commitment** across multiple mayors
   - Inclusive governance - involved community in planning
   - Tied social services to security improvements

4. **Economic Development:**
   - Fostered local businesses, tourism
   - Destigmatized poor neighborhoods through branding

**Costs:**
- **Financial:** Hundreds of millions over decades (exact figures unclear, but infrastructure projects expensive)
- **Time:** 15-25 years to achieve transformation
- **Human:** Continued violence during transition period

### Timeline:
- **1991:** Peak violence (Escobar era)
- **1993:** Escobar killed by security forces
- **2004-2011:** Major Social Urbanism projects (mayors Sergio Fajardo, Alonso Salazar)
- **2010s:** Violence stabilized at low levels
- **Present:** Violence creeping back up (gangs re-establishing control in some areas)

### What Worked:
1. **Combination of security + social investment** (not either/or)
2. **Physical integration** - Connected isolated areas reduces gang territorial control
3. **Community pride** - Quality public spaces gave residents stake in peace
4. **Political continuity** - 15+ years of consistent approach (rare!)
5. **Addressing root causes** - Created alternatives to gang recruitment

### What Failed/Limitations:
1. **Violence not eliminated** - "Tense calm" remains, gangs still present
2. **High costs** - Critics note enormous expense
3. **Requires stable political environment** - Multiple mayors stayed the course (unlikely in many contexts)
4. **Gentrification concerns** - Some residents displaced by development
5. **Recent backsliding** - Gangs re-asserting control in some areas as investment wanes
6. **External factors critical** - Demobilization of paramilitaries, end of Escobar cartel happened independently

### Prerequisites for Replication:
- Minimum level of security (enough to build infrastructure)
- Political stability and long-term commitment (15+ years)
- Significant financial resources (hundreds of millions)
- Technical capacity for urban planning/construction
- Community engagement mechanisms
- Functional local government

### Applicability to Haiti:
**Potential:**
- Addresses root causes (unemployment, isolation, lack of opportunity)
- Creates positive alternative to gangs for youth
- Model has evidence of sustained success (with caveats)

**Challenges:**
- Medellín had functioning city government (Haiti's national government barely functions)
- Requires baseline security to build infrastructure (Haiti lacks this)
- Needs 15-20 year political commitment (Haiti has transitional council, no long-term stability)
- Expensive (Haiti has no budget, depends on foreign aid)
- Medellín's success partly due to external factors (Escobar killed, paramilitaries demobilized independently)
- Cable cars to poor neighborhoods require... electricity and maintenance (Haiti lacks both)

**Verdict:** **Excellent long-term model IF security baseline established first + political stability + massive investment. Not applicable in current Haiti chaos.**

## Case 3: Liberia - DDR (Disarmament, Demobilization, Reintegration) Post-Civil War (2003-2004)

### Context
- 14-year civil war (1989-2003), multiple armed factions
- Estimated 200,000+ dead, country devastated
- UNMIL (UN peacekeeping) deployed 15,000 troops

### What Happened (Facts Only):
**Results:**
- **Successfully disarmed >100,000 former fighters** (Dec 2003 - Nov 2004)
- **11,000 child soldiers disarmed** and enrolled in rehabilitation
- **Program considered a DDR success** by UN standards
- Liberia has remained relatively stable since (no return to civil war)

**Methods:**
1. **Disarmament:**
   - Fighters surrendered weapons in exchange for cash ($300) + benefits
   - UN peacekeepers secured disarmament sites
   - Weapons destroyed publicly

2. **Demobilization:**
   - Fighters registered, documented
   - Temporary camps for processing
   - Medical screening, basic services

3. **Reintegration:**
   - Vocational training programs (skills development)
   - Educational opportunities (school enrollment for youth)
   - Psychological counseling (trauma treatment)
   - Community acceptance programs (reconciliation)

**Costs:**
- **Financial:** Hundreds of millions (UNMIL + DDR program)
- **Time:** 1 year for D&D, multi-year for reintegration
- **Personnel:** 15,000 UN peacekeepers

### Timeline:
- **2003:** Peace agreement signed, UNMIL deployed
- **Dec 2003 - Nov 2004:** Disarmament and demobilization
- **2004-2008+:** Reintegration phase (ongoing)
- **2006:** Ellen Johnson Sirleaf elected (first female African head of state)
- **2018:** Liberia still stable, no return to large-scale violence

### What Worked:
1. **Strong UN peacekeeping presence** - 15,000 troops provided security
2. **Financial incentives** - Cash + benefits gave fighters reason to disarm
3. **Comprehensive approach** - Not just weapons buyback, but reintegration support
4. **Child soldier focus** - Special programs for youth
5. **Political will** - All warring parties agreed to peace process
6. **Community-based reintegration** - Involved local communities in accepting ex-combatants

### What Failed/Limitations:
1. **Reintegration success mixed** - Many ex-combatants struggled to find employment
2. **Some weapons hidden** - Not all arms surrendered
3. **Justice limited** - Few war criminals prosecuted (trade-off for peace)
4. **Ongoing poverty** - Root causes (economic desperation) not fully addressed
5. **Regional instability** - Some ex-combatants fought in neighboring conflicts (Côte d'Ivoire)
6. **Dependency on international support** - Local capacity weak

### Prerequisites for Replication:
- **Peace agreement among warring parties** (must ALL agree to disarm)
- **Large international peacekeeping force** (15,000 troops for 3M population)
- **Financial resources** for DDR packages (hundreds of millions)
- **Alternative livelihoods** for ex-combatants (jobs, training, education)
- **Community reconciliation mechanisms** (accept former fighters back)
- **Political transition** (new government with legitimacy)

### Applicability to Haiti:
**Potential:**
- Proven model for disarming large numbers (100,000 combatants comparable to Haiti's 12-20k gang members)
- Child focus relevant (Haiti gangs ~70% children)
- Community-based approach could work in Haiti's neighborhoods

**Challenges:**
- **No peace agreement** - Haitian gangs have no incentive to disarm (they're winning)
- **No credible security guarantees** - Who protects disarmed gang members from rivals?
- **Limited alternative livelihoods** - Haiti economy can't absorb ex-gang members (no jobs)
- **Different conflict type** - Liberia was political war with clear end. Haiti is criminal economy with ongoing revenue.
- **Gang leaders profit too much** - Extortion generates $60-75M/year. Why disarm?
- **No unified gang command** - Can't negotiate with 200+ fragmented gangs
- **International force insufficient** - Current MSS mission only ~3,000 personnel (Liberia had 15,000)

**Verdict:** **DDR model won't work without first defeating gangs militarily. Gangs must lose before they'll negotiate. Currently they're winning.**

## Case 4: Rwanda - Security Sector Reform Post-Genocide (1994-Present)

### Context
- 1994 genocide: 800,000+ killed in 100 days
- Complete state collapse, military/police disbanded
- RPF (Tutsi rebels) took power, rebuilt from scratch

### What Happened (Facts Only):
**Results:**
- **Built functional security forces from zero** 
- **Rwanda now has low crime rates** (official statistics show dramatic drops)
- **Strong state capacity** - police/military loyal, professional
- **Authoritarian model** - Ruled by Paul Kagame since 1994

**Methods:**
1. **Complete rebuild** - Started from scratch, didn't try to reform old forces
2. **Strict vetting** - Excluded genocide perpetrators, built loyalist forces
3. **Community policing** - Local defense forces (Gacaca courts for justice)
4. **Ideological training** - National unity, anti-corruption messaging
5. **Zero tolerance** - Harsh penalties for crime/corruption
6. **Surveillance state** - Extensive monitoring, limited freedom

**Costs:**
- **Human rights:** Authoritarian control, limited political freedom
- **Time:** 10+ years to build capacity
- **Financial:** Billions in international aid + domestic investment

### Timeline:
- **1994:** Genocide ends, RPF takes power
- **1994-2000:** Rebuilding security forces, establishing control
- **2000s:** Professionalization of police/military
- **2010s-present:** Stable security, economic growth, but authoritarian rule

### What Worked:
1. **Clean slate** - Didn't try to reform corrupt institutions
2. **Strong leadership** - Kagame maintained control, prevented infighting
3. **International support** - Aid flowed due to genocide guilt
4. **Meritocracy in security forces** - Promoted based on competence
5. **Community involvement** - Local mechanisms complemented state security

### What Failed/Limitations:
1. **Authoritarian model** - Not democratic, limited freedoms
2. **Ethnic tensions suppressed, not resolved** - Tutsi-dominated government
3. **Regional instability** - Rwanda accused of destabilizing Congo
4. **Not replicable in democratic contexts** - Requires strongman rule

### Prerequisites for Replication:
- Complete state collapse (allows clean slate)
- Strong unifying leader with military control
- International aid and support
- Willingness to accept authoritarian model
- Ethnic/ideological glue to unite security forces

### Applicability to Haiti:
**Potential:**
- Clean slate approach might be necessary (current police compromised)
- Community-based security resonates with Haiti's local structures

**Challenges:**
- **No Kagame equivalent** - Haiti has no unifying leader with military control
- **No unifying ideology** - Rwanda had genocide trauma as nation-building glue. Haiti has... what?
- **Democratic expectations** - International community won't support overt authoritarianism
- **Regional differences** - Rwanda is landlocked, easier to control borders. Haiti has coastline, porous DR border.
- **Economic base** - Rwanda had agriculture, minerals. Haiti has remittances and... not much else.

**Verdict:** **Rwanda model requires authoritarian strongman + complete state collapse + international backing. Haiti has the collapse, but no strongman and no international appetite for authoritarianism.**

## Case 5: Jamaica - Kingston Garrison Politics (Partial Failure Case Study)

### Context
- Urban gangs tied to political parties (JLP vs PNP)
- "Garrison communities" controlled by gangs funded by politicians
- Similar to Haiti's elite-gang nexus

### What Happened:
**Results:**
- **Violence persists** - Jamaica still has high homicide rate (~50/100k)
- **Political-criminal nexus NOT broken** - Gangs still tied to parties
- **Some progress in specific areas** - Tivoli Gardens raid (2010) disrupted Shower Posse gang

**Methods:**
- Military raids on gang strongholds (2010 Tivoli operation: 73 civilians killed)
- Anti-gang laws (ZOSO - Zones of Special Operations)
- Economic development programs (limited success)
- Police reform efforts (ongoing)

### What Didn't Work:
1. **Military raids without political reform** - Gangs reconstitute
2. **Didn't break elite-gang nexus** - Politicians still fund gangs for votes
3. **No sustained political will** - Reforms abandoned after elections
4. **Economic alternatives insufficient** - Youth still recruited into gangs

### Lessons for Haiti:
- **Must break elite-gang nexus** - Security operations alone won't work if elites keep funding gangs
- **Military raids create martyrs** - Tivoli raid killed 73 civilians, strengthened gang narrative
- **Need political reform + security** - Can't have one without the other

**Verdict:** **Jamaica shows what NOT to do - don't raid gangs while politicians still fund them. Break the elite nexus first.**

## Synthesis: What Actually Works?

### Success Factors (Evidence-Based):
1. **Multi-pronged approach** - Security + economic + political reform (Medellín model)
2. **Sustained commitment** - 15-20 years minimum (Medellín, Rwanda)
3. **Break political-criminal nexus** - Elites can't keep funding gangs (Jamaica's failure)
4. **Overwhelming force when needed** - But must be followed by reintegration (El Salvador, Liberia)
5. **Alternative livelihoods** - Youth need non-gang options (all successful cases)
6. **Community involvement** - Top-down alone doesn't work (Medellín, Rwanda, Liberia)
7. **Political stability** - Can't succeed with rotating weak governments (Medellín had continuity)

### Failure Patterns:
1. **Security alone** - Doesn't address root causes, gangs return (El Salvador long-term TBD)
2. **Economic development alone** - Requires minimum security first (can't build if gangs shoot)
3. **DDR without defeating gangs first** - Won't work if gangs are winning (Liberia only worked after war ENDED)
4. **Reform without breaking elite ties** - Elites will sabotage (Jamaica's ongoing failure)
5. **Short-term interventions** - 2-3 year missions accomplish nothing (MINUSTAH learned this)

## Haiti-Specific Recommendations Based on Comparative Evidence:

### Phase 1: Security (El Salvador-style, with modifications)
**Goal:** Break gang territorial control, disrupt command structures  
**Duration:** 1-2 years  
**Requirements:**
- 20,000+ international troops (not current 3,000 MSS force)
- Massive prison capacity (build new facilities)
- Strict ROE to minimize civilian casualties (learn from El Salvador's mistakes)
- Simultaneous action against elite gang funders (asset freezes, arrests)

**Trade-offs:** 
- Human rights risks (mitigate with oversight, release innocent quickly)
- International criticism (accept as cost)

### Phase 2: DDR (Liberia-style)
**Goal:** Disarm gang members, begin reintegration  
**Duration:** 2-3 years  
**Requirements:**
- Peace holds from Phase 1 (gangs defeated)
- $150-300 per surrendered weapon
- Vocational training, job placement programs
- Psychological services for trauma

**Trade-offs:**
- Some gang members escape justice (prioritize peace over punishment)
- Expensive ($500M+ for 15-20k gang members)

### Phase 3: Social Urbanism (Medellín-style)
**Goal:** Address root causes, create alternatives to gang life  
**Duration:** 15-20 years  
**Requirements:**
- $2-3 billion investment in infrastructure
- Political stability (new constitution, legitimate government)
- Technical capacity (urban planning, construction)
- Community engagement

**Trade-offs:**
- Generational timeline (no quick fixes)
- Risk of elite capture (international oversight needed)

### Phase 4: Institutional Reform (Rwanda-lite)
**Goal:** Build functional state capacity  
**Duration:** 10-15 years  
**Requirements:**
- Rebuild police from scratch (current force compromised)
- Judicial reform (clear case backlog, build courts)
- Anti-corruption (vet all officials, prosecute elite corruption)

**Trade-offs:**
- May require authoritarian measures short-term (but must transition to democracy)

## Critical Insights for Haiti:

1. **No single model works alone** - Haiti needs hybrid approach
2. **Security first, but not security only** - Must transition to development quickly
3. **Break elite-gang nexus immediately** - Or all other efforts will be sabotaged
4. **20-year commitment minimum** - Anything less is performative
5. **International community must stay** - But hand over to Haitians gradually
6. **Learn from El Salvador's human rights abuses** - Don't repeat them
7. **Learn from Medellín's long-term vision** - But accept it requires stability first
8. **Learn from Liberia's DDR** - But recognize it only worked AFTER war ended
9. **Learn from Jamaica's failure** - Political-criminal nexus must be broken
